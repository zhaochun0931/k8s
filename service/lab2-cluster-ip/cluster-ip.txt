1. How ClusterIP Works
When you use a ClusterIP (the default), Kubernetes gives the Service a single, stable IP address.

When you connect to postgres, you talk to that one IP.

The Service then "load balances" your request to any available Postgres Pod.



A ClusterIP supports load balancing because it doesn't actually exist as a physical device. Instead, it is a Virtual IP (VIP) managed by a process called kube-proxy that lives on every node in your cluster.

1. The "How": How it Works (The Traffic Cop)
When you create a ClusterIP service, Kubernetes does three things:

Assigns an IP: It gives the service a "Virtual IP" from a internal pool.

Identifies Backends: It looks at your selector (e.g., app: nginx) and finds all Pods with that label. These are called Endpoints.

Configures the Nodes: The kube-proxy on every node writes "Rules" (using iptables or IPVS) into the Linux kernel of the node.

When your packet hits the ClusterIP, the Linux kernel looks at its rules and says: "If a packet is going to 10.96.0.10 (Service), intercept it and send it to 10.244.0.5 (Pod A) instead."






Standard vs. Headless (Load Balance View)
Standard (ClusterIP): The Cluster handles the load balancing. You talk to one IP, and the "rules" decide which Pod answers.

Headless: The Client handles the load balancing. Kubernetes gives you all the IPs, and you (the code or user) decide which one to connect to.
