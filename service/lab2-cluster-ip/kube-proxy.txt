1. The Probability Math (Fixed Length)

RULE #    PROBABILITY    REMAINING TRAFFIC    ACTUAL CHANCE    TARGET POD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
1         0.33333        100%                 ~33%             10.244.0.10
2         0.50000        66%                  ~33%             10.244.0.11
3         (Always)       33%                  ~33%             10.244.0.9
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Rule 1: Takes 1/3 of all incoming requests and sends them to Pod .10.

Rule 2: Of the 2/3 of traffic that "fell through" Rule 1, it takes half (0.50000). Half of 2/3 is exactly 1/3.

Rule 3: This is the "catch-all." Since there is no probability listed, it takes 100% of whatever is left.


2. Decoding the Terms for your .txt Lab Notes

KUBE-SVC-xxx: The "Service" chain. This is the entry point for your ClusterIP (10.96.12.55).

KUBE-SEP-xxx: "Service End Point." These are the specific rules for your individual Pods.

KUBE-MARK-MASQ: This ensures that if traffic comes from outside the cluster, the return packet knows how to find its way back (Masquerading/SNAT).

statistic mode random: This tells the Linux kernel to use the random number generator to decide if this rule should "fire."





3. Why this is "Invisible" to the App
When your tester pod ran curl 10.96.12.55, it thought it was talking to a single server. It had no idea that the Linux Kernel was intercepting that packet and rewriting the destination IP to 10.244.0.11 before it even left the virtual network card!







1. The "Traffic Splitter" (Load Balancing)
These lines explain why your curl hits different Pods. The kernel evaluates these rules top-to-bottom:

Rule 1 (Probability 0.33): The kernel rolls a "dice." If it hits 1–33, the traffic goes to Pod 10.244.0.10. If not, it moves to the next rule.

Rule 2 (Probability 0.50): Of the remaining 66% of traffic, the kernel rolls again. Since 50% of 66% is 33%, another third of total traffic goes to Pod 10.244.0.11.

Rule 3 (No Probability): This is the "else" statement. 100% of whatever is left (the final third) goes to Pod 10.244.0.9.

2. The Columns Explained (Fixed Length)
3. What is KUBE-MARK-MASQ?
You see this line at the very top:

KUBE-MARK-MASQ  tcp  -- !10.244.0.0/16   10.96.12.55

The ! means NOT. This rule says: "If the traffic is coming from OUTSIDE the cluster (not from 10.244.x.x) and heading to our Service IP (10.96.12.55), mark it for Masquerading."

This is critical because it tells the network: "When the Pod replies, send the answer back through the Service IP so the outside world recognizes the sender." Without this, the outside client would get a reply from a "random" Pod IP it didn't ask for and would reject the connection.

4. Why is this cool?
Because this is happening at the Kernel level, it is incredibly fast. Your application code doesn't have to know anything about load balancing. It just sends a packet to 10.96.12.55, and the Linux kernel "teleports" that packet to the correct Pod before it even touches the physical wire.
